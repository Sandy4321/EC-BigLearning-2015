Big Learning Activity

This activity is part of the 2015 Evolutionary Computation for Big Data and Big Learning workshop held within GECCO. We invite everyone to participate, regardless of whether the participant will assist to the workshop.


The goal:

The goal of the activity is to discover features that will help us predict low blood pressure events (Acute Hypotension Episodes or AHEs) in ICU patients.

Definition:

We define an AHE as a period of 1 minute where the average of the mean arterial blood pressure (MAP) is below 58 mm Hg. Note that MAP is not simply the average of the blood pressure level. The ALFA@MIT team precomputed these values to detect AHEs and therefore label blood pressure time series.


The dataset:

We propose a binary classification problem where:

  • The associated dataset is composed of 694058 labeled examples
  • The examples are retrieved from 100 patients
  • Each example corresponds to ten seconds of continuous monitoring of a single patient's blood pressure. The sampling frequency is 125Hz, therefore each example is composed of 1250 samples.
  • The examples do not contain acute hypotension episodes. This means that all the examples qualify as "normal" at the time of the blood pressure measurement.
  • The labels indicate the state of the patient 10 minutes later: label 0 indicates a "normal" blood pressure (10 minutes later) while label 1 indicates an AHE (again 10 minutes later).
  • There are 12000 positive examples and 682058 negative examples. Therefore, positive examples represent 1.73% of the data.

Why AHE?

Among the most critical events that occur in intensive care units (ICUs), acute hypotensive episodes require effective, prompt intervention. Left untreated, such episodes may result in irreversible organ damage and death. Timely and appropriate interventions can reduce these risks. Determining what intervention is appropriate in any given case depends on diagnosing the cause of the episode, which might be sepsis, myocardial infarction, cardiac arrhythmia, pulmonary embolism, hemorrhage, dehydration, anaphylaxis, effects of medication, or any of a wide variety of other causes of hypovolemia, insufficient cardiac output, or vasodilatory shock. Often the best choice may be a suboptimal but relatively safe intervention, simply to buy enough time to select a more effective treatment without exposing the patient to the additional risks of delaying treatment (Herman Foobar, 2009)
